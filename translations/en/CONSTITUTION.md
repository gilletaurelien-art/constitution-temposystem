# THE CONSTITUTION OF THE DIGITAL RIGHTS OF THE PERSON

**Our rights, engraved before the first line of code. By signing, you join this “we”.**

*Version 2.0 — July 2026 — a living text, written in a single voice: a constitution is never finished.*
*English translation of the canonical French text ([CONSTITUTION.md](../../CONSTITUTION.md), v2.0).*

---

> **All technology exercises power over our lives.**
> **We, the people of the digital age, here set the limits that this power shall not cross.**

---

## Preamble

Every day, we entrust machines with fragments of our lives: our words, our bonds, our memories, our attention. These machines are not neutral — they shape what we see, influence what we do, and keep the record of who we are. Such power calls for a limit. That limit **is us**.

This text is not a list of good intentions. It states the **rights we demand**, and the renunciations that those who build our tools impose upon themselves to honour them: renouncing the capture of our attention, the selling of who we are, the leveraging of our regret, our lock-in, and the measuring of our rest as a fault.

This text is written in a single voice — ours. Every article belongs to us. Each of us may claim it, quote it, and hold it up against anyone who presumes to decide in our place. A digital economy can prosper without turning our intimacy into merchandise; here we state the conditions.

---

## Article 1 — On the nature of our data

Each of our data points represents a part of one of us: a word, a relationship, a work. As such, **our data is not an asset**. It is neither a raw material nor a mere economic resource. Holding it creates a duty towards us — never a property right over us.

This principle precedes everything else. What follows is not a list of favours granted to us: it is the direct consequence of what our data is.

## Title I — On our person

**Article 2 — On our dignity.** Each of us is an end, never a means: not a resource to extract, not a profile to exploit, not a metric to maximise. No goal — commercial, technical or strategic — justifies manipulating us, engineering our dependency, watching us in secret or preying on our fragilities.

**Article 3 — On our sovereignty.** We remain masters of our data. Each of us may veil it from others without destroying our own memory, refuse to appear in a collective work — even one already paid for by a third party — and erase what concerns us. Our right always comes before the right of the transaction.

**Article 4 — On our leaving.** Each of us may leave whenever we choose, taking all of our data in an open, documented, reusable format. No technical lock will hold us: we stay out of attachment, never out of captivity.

**Article 5 — On our protection when we are fragile.** Children, elders, the bereaved, the isolated, the ill, or simply the fragile: when one of us is vulnerable, they are protected more, never exploited more — dedicated infrastructures; no scoring, no comparison, no exposure. Our vulnerability will never be a lever to convert us. And those of us who grew up here as minors receive, on coming of age, the history of their digital life in full sovereignty — a passage of dignity, not a mere change of technical permission.

## Title II — On our rhythms

**Article 6 — On our attention.** Our attention is not a raw material. Notifications, recommendations and reminders must serve a need that is truly ours, never the obsession with making us return, click or stay. A good tool increases the value of the time we choose, not the length of the time it takes from us.

**Article 7 — On our silence and our rest.** Doing nothing is a right. No system will read our silence as a lack, a debt or a problem to fix, nor chase after those of us who have simply been still. We have the right to be absent; it is for the tools to respect our rhythms, not for us to follow theirs. **A humane system does not panic at our silence.**

**Article 8 — On the asymmetry of attention.** A gesture one of us offers another may be made visible by a gentle signal. The absence of a gesture will never be displayed, counted or inferred: presence may be celebrated; absence may never be pointed at. Relational-debt counters, forced read receipts and presence scores have no place among us.

**Article 9 — Against manipulation.** No interface will push us to act against our own interest, hide the way to refuse, complicate our leaving, or make the healthiest choice less visible than the most profitable one. Our regret will never be played upon: fear of missing out, guilt and manufactured urgency are not instruments of growth. An agreement extracted from our fatigue is not consent.

## Title III — On our memory

**Article 10 — On the inalienability of our past.** Access to our essential bonds and to the raw history of our digital lives will never depend on a payment, an expiry threat or a punitive deletion. Space for our tomorrow may be sold to us; the survival of our yesterday will never be threatened. **Our memory is not for ransom.**

**Article 11 — On non-commodification.** Only the minimum necessary is collected from us; retention is reasonable and its durations are explained to us. Our data is neither sold, nor rented, nor handed over for advertising. None of our content trains an artificial intelligence without our explicit, specific and revocable consent: the machine assists us; it never owns our stories.

**Article 12 — On the economy that respects us.** We pay for a service genuinely rendered — a crafting, an edition, an object, an accompaniment — never for mere access to our own bonds. Value comes from the work added, not from the silent exploitation of who we are.

## Title IV — On what we demand

**Article 13 — On our right to understand.** Any automated decision that weighs on one of us must be explainable to them in clear language. We know when a machine intervenes, by which main logics, and what we can do to contest it.

**Article 14 — On accountability that proves itself.** A good intention is not enough: it must show in the architecture, the data, the metrics and the budget trade-offs. **Benevolence is not a marketing tone: it is a technical constraint.** Growth and engagement remain subordinate to our trust, our clarity and the lightening of our mental load. The silence of any one of us is never measured against them.

**Article 15 — On honesty.** Nothing is presented to us as done until it is built. A promise of security, encryption or permanence is displayed as a dated commitment, never as an accomplished fact. Gaps between word and practice are disclosed to us publicly, along with the measures taken to close them.

**Article 16 — On our recourse, and on this text that is ours.** Any system can harm, even unintentionally: we always have simple ways to report, contest, understand and obtain redress. Every major evolution is judged by its effects on us — who gains power, who becomes exposed, who can leave, who pays the true price. And this Constitution is ours: we may adopt it, amend it, translate it, criticise it, fork it. It only truly protects us if we take hold of it.

---

## Conclusion

Another digital world is possible: one where our data is no longer merchandise, where our trust is worth more than our capture, where our silence is respected, where we remain sovereign.

A truly humane system is judged not only by what it lets us do, but by what it refuses to do to us. No innovation deserves the name of progress if it requires us to become smaller so that it may become greater.

Because behind every piece of data there is one of us — and no business model should ever forget it.

## Lineage — this text does not stand alone

This Constitution joins a family of texts which, all over the world, strive to put the person back at the centre of the digital world:

1. [European Declaration on Digital Rights and Principles](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/european-declaration-digital-rights-and-principles) — European Union, 2022.
2. [The Copenhagen Letter](https://copenhagenletter.org) — 150 makers of tech, 2017.
3. [Contract for the Web](https://contractfortheweb.org) — Tim Berners-Lee, 2019.
