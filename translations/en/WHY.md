# WHY

*This document is not about a product, an organisation, or their authors. It tells why a digital constitution has become necessary — so that whoever opens this repository in fifty years understands not only the articles, but their reason for existing.*

---

## Five ages of the network

The internet was first a **documentary network**: we consulted it. Software was a library.

It became a **social network**: we connected through it. Human attention became the resource, and software learned to hold it.

It became an **economic network**: we traded on it. The traces of human lives became a balance-sheet asset, and software learned to sell them.

It became an **algorithmic network**: the machine began choosing what each of us sees, reads, believes. Software ceased to be a tool we use and became an environment we inhabit.

It has become a **network of intelligences**: systems write, recommend, decide and remember on our behalf. They are trained on the traces of our lives, and their choices now affect memory, relationships, attention, work, health, grief.

At every age, software gained a power. At no age did it receive any limits other than the market's — that is to say, none.

## The moment a constitution becomes necessary

History teaches that unlimited power always meets its limits eventually — but late, and after the damage. The classic restraining mechanisms arrive afterwards: law is slow and territorial while technology is fast and global; professional ethics presuppose constituted professions, when anyone can deploy a system touching millions of people.

Between the law that has not yet arrived and the market that stops nothing, one actor remains: **those who build**. A constitution of artisans waits for no legislator and no regulator. It is made of voluntary renunciations, immediately applicable, and verifiable — not in speeches, but in architectures, data schemas and chosen metrics.

## Why now

Three shifts converged in the mid-2020s.

**The attention economy reached maturity.** It is no longer measured in page views but in hours of human lives, and its techniques — artificial urgency, guilt, fear of missing out — have become a taught, tooled, normalised state of the art.

**Artificial intelligences began learning from human traces.** For the first time, people's memories, writings and voices are no longer merely stored: they are ingested, becoming the substance of other systems, without the persons having wanted it or even known it.

**Human memory changed medium.** Entire generations now live with a memory — photos, messages, voices, bonds — entrusted wholesale to private infrastructures. For the first time in history, the continuity of families' memory depends on business models.

When attention, machine learning and the memory of the living depend on the same actors, with no declared limits, the constitutional moment has arrived.

## The central gesture

Every era has had to remove certain things from commerce — to decide they would no longer be goods, whatever their market value. That is always a change of category, never a mere regulation.

The central gesture of this Constitution is of that nature: **human data is not an asset.** Not "deserving more protection" — *of another nature*. Once the category changes, selling, profiling and capture cease to be business-model choices: they become questions of responsibility towards persons.

## Writing to last

The articles of this Constitution do not describe technologies: they describe **human limits** — attention, rest, memory, dignity, bonds — which change infinitely more slowly than tools.

That is their quality criterion: if an article cannot be applied to a technology that does not yet exist, it is badly written. If, in thirty years, someone can apply the right to rest to a system none of us can imagine, then this text will have done its work — not describing tools, but holding limits.
